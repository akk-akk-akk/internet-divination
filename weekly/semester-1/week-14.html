I got Zarer and Alda to try the website, and got a few feedback points from them. The overall feedback was that the website was not intuitive enough, and there was too much text. Feedback from Andreas

When I showed Andreas the website, he questioned the accuracy of the model. We tested a few images, and most of them were showing "My Sources Say No..." as the answer. I wondered why this happened because all the images that the model was trained on were truly random. I decided to consult Blackbox AI.

Question: My machine learning model is trained on 30 images of 1000x1000px, each pixel is randomly assigned a black or white color, creating a truly random imgae. There are 3 classes and each class is trained on 10 images. However, I seem to get the result "my sources say no" more than the other two. Why could that be?

It suggested some possibilities:

Class Imbalance: Even though you trained on 10 images per class, there might be some subtle imbalance in the data that's causing the model to favor one class over others.

Random Chance: Given that your images are truly random, it's possible that by chance, the "my sources say no" class had patterns that were more distinctive or easier for the model to learn.

Model Overfitting: With only 30 images total, there's a high risk of overfitting. The model might have latched onto specific patterns in the "my sources say no" images that aren't actually meaningful.

Threshold Effects: The model might be more confident in its "my sources say no" predictions, leading to more frequent selection of this class when applying classification thresholds.

Input Processing: If there's any preprocessing of the input images before classification that differs from the training process, it could inadvertently favor one class.

Model Architecture: The specific architecture of your model might be more prone to detecting features present in the "my sources say no" images.

Random Initialization: The initial random weights of the model could have led to a bias towards one class that persisted through training.

Testing Data Distribution: If you're testing on a small number of images, you might be seeing a temporary bias in results that would even out with more tests.



Overall, I think this was a great exercise into initial user testing and getting feedback for the website. I was able to identify a few problems that I could work on from here.

A few changes I intend of making are:
1. Overall, rethink the user experience and the amount of text it takes for them to understand the concept. I want to experiment with the experiment as a physical interactive experience, maybe with a camera.
2. Test the model again to see what the problem is, and why one result is more common than another.

Extension for Manifestation

I stumbled onto an article that talked about a trend of the Manifinsta.



I thought back to my writings about the ritual back in week 4. I believe this could be viewed as a modern "superstition" in which we get back what we put out into the universe (in this case, on the internet).

Some interesting quotes from the article:

“There’s a culture of self-deprecation online that I’ve started to reject”

"As young people continue to turn away from traditional organised religions, ideas and techniques like manifestation are ultimately getting picked up by Silicon Valley-type apps and companies."

"While posting that your job interview will go well or that your ex-boyfriend still definitely “needs you” to ten people are harmless, the premise that everything you consume and engage with online builds your future reality is terrifying."

This is yet another example of a well-meaning trend that finds its roots in our old divination practices. The article warns of the risks of such trends, as they still allow insidious advertisements to use the data we put up for its own gain. It seems only a matter of time before our personal manifestations are used by algorithms to sell us a product, or used to manipulate us in other ways.

This begs the question, is there a way we can create a self-contained manifestation portal that doesn't rely on the internet?

Glance Back

I was reminded of Maya Man's project 'Glance Back' that I found a while ago. The chrome extension gets the user to take a photo of themselves at random moments of the day and type a short caption of what they were doing at the moment.

The extension served as little interventions through the day to take the user out of the trance that we often fall into while browsing the internet. Man stressed the importance that the images were self-contained and saved to the local storage, making the experience one purely shared by the user and the computer.

SFPC_Malware_Anthology.zip

I also remembered seeing a series of projects by School for Poetic Computation students in which they created a series of "viruses" as interventions. The idea of a virus infiltrating the experience of a user, but turned on its head for positive interventions was so genius to me, and I wanted to see if I could create my own version of that.
